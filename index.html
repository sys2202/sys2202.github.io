Basic Course Information

Course Description:

This course

  • Provides students with the background necessary to model, store, manipulate, and exchange information throughout an information system to support decision-making;
  • Incorporates both conceptual bases and corresponding technology standards, including the Unified Modeling Language (UML), Entity Relationship Diagrams, SQL, and others;
  • Covers the development of conceptual (semantic) models for describing data and their relationships, relational models, effective use of SQL for data definition and manipulation, web-based technologies for disseminating information, and the major components of modern information systems; and
  • Emphasizes the application of these technologies through analyzing, designing, and implementing web-enabled database systems.

Learning Objectives:

By the end of this course, you will be able to

  • Articulate fundamental data and information engineering concepts and apply them to systems engineering problems;
  • Model data using entity-relationship diagrams (ERDs) and the Unified Modeling Language (UML);
  • Deploy relational databases to house, serve, and integrate data in an efficient and integrity-preserving fashion;
  • Effectively use SQL, Python, and data interchange formats (e.g., JSON) to model, store, process, and explore data;
  • Analyze the basic quantitative and qualitative properties of data;
  • Display the properties of data that are most important for the decision at hand;
  • Access, configure, and use cloud-based services; and
  • Construct a data engineering pipeline for a real-world application.

Prerequisites:

SYS 2001, CS 1110, CS 2110, SYS 3501, SIE Major.

Textbook:

No required textbook to purchase, but below texts will be referenced and students are encouraged to read chapters from:

  • Fundamentals of Data Engineering by Joe Reis and Matt Housley
  • SQL Cookbook by Anthony Molinaro and Robert de Graaf
  • Python for Data Science Handbook by Jake VanderPlas.
  • Matplotlib: Visualization with Python

Course Components

Lectures:

Unless otherwise planned, lectures will be held on Tuesdays. The lecture time will be divided into the following parts:

  • 5-10 minutes to review common questions and themes from past lectures.
  • 30-40 minutes to present new content using a combination of demonstrations, whiteboard explanations, and projected slides.
  • 10-15 minutes for in-class activities related to the topic.
  • 10-15 minutes to complete the reflection quiz.
  • 5-minute break in the middle if time allows.

Reflection Quizzes (RQs):

After each lecture, you will complete a reflection quiz. The quiz will contain questions that require you to reflect upon what you learned during the lecture, what topics you still have questions about, anything that helped or hindered your learning, and 3-5 short-answer questions about the lecture topic. Please keep in mind that the closed questions for the midterm and final exams will have the same format as the RQ questions. RQs are intended to be completed by the end of class. If you miss the lecture, you can complete the RQ by reviewing the lecture slides, recordings (if available), and resources. To receive RQ completion credit, you must submit it by Wednesday at 23:59. To receive full credit, complete 8 RQs.

Labs:

Unless stated otherwise, we will have hands-on lab sessions every Thursday. These labs will be roughly divided into two parts. In the first part, cases and examples related to the current week’s topic and/or previous assignments are provided, and you follow along on your laptop. Lab exercises will often span multiple weeks, and we will work on a new chunk each week as we learn more concepts and techniques. In the second part of the lab, you will work on a new assignment independently or with your classmates while receiving assistance from the instructors and TAs. Labs are intended to be completed by the end of class. If you miss attending the lab for any reason, including illness, you must complete it on your own. To receive lab completion credit, this lab assignment must be completed and submitted on Canvas by Friday at 23:59. To receive full credit, complete 8 labs.

Hands-on Exercises:

SYS2202 is a hands-on course, making practice essential beyond class time. We will use DataCamp, an interactive platform, to allow you to practice and review some of the topics covered in the course. You will receive an invitation to join DataCamp, which you must accept immediately. Please ensure you can access and navigate the platform, and if you face any issues, please inform us immediately. Your grade will be based on the number of points earned and the completion of the assignments. To receive full credit, you must earn a minimum of 20,000 XP. There are 5 optional assignments for extra credit, due on the last day of class. You can earn an extra credit for an additional 6,000 XP beyond the requirement.

Exams:

Exams are based entirely on classroom material, discussions, readings, and labs. Part A of the exam will contain a closed-book section with short answer questions, and Part B will contain an open-book section requiring analytical problem-solving.

Course Project:

For your course project, you will develop a data pipeline using Python for a topic of your choice. We highly recommend you choose a topic that interests you, and we are happy to help you select a data source and define the project. You can work on the project in a group of 3-5 students. You must submit a topic description and data sources for your final project on Canvas on the specified date. Please plan to identify your teammates and topic of interest within a couple weeks of the semester's beginning. The project deliverables are due on the last day of the course. More information will be available at the midpoint of the semester.

    The Use of Generative AI:

    Generative artificial intelligence tools—software that creates new text, images, computer code, audio, video, and other content—have become widely available. Well-known examples include ChatGPT for text and DALL•E for images. This policy governs all such tools, including those released during our semester together. You may use generative AI tools on assignments in this course when I explicitly permit you to do so. Otherwise, you should refrain from using such tools.

    If you do use generative AI tools on assignments in this class, you must properly document and credit the tools themselves. Cite the tool you used, following the pattern for computer software given in the specified style guide. Additionally, please include a brief description of how you used the tool. If you choose to use generative AI tools, please remember that they are typically trained on limited datasets that may be out of date. Additionally, generative AI datasets are trained on pre-existing material, including copyrighted material; therefore, relying on a generative AI tool may result in plagiarism or copyright violations.

    Finally, keep in mind that the goal of generative AI tools is to produce content that seems to have been produced by a human, not to produce accurate or reliable content; therefore, relying on a generative AI tool may result in your submission of inaccurate content. It is your responsibility—not the tool's—to assure the quality, integrity, and accuracy of work you submit in any college course. If you use generative AI tools to complete assignments in this course, in ways that I have not explicitly authorized, I reserve the right to apply the Honor Code to your specific case. In addition, you must be wary of unintentional plagiarism or fabrication of data. Please act with integrity, for the sake of both your personal character and your academic record.

    In this course, the following policy dictates the acceptable use of generative AI tools, such as ChatGPT and other similar technologies. This is a programming course and while I recognize that generative AI can be a tremendous learning tool, it is my expectation that these technologies do not impede you learning the programming concepts taught in class. However, these technologies can be used to enhance your learning experience much like one would use Stack Overflow on assignments with the exception of on exams and lab quizzes which must be utilized without generative AI.
